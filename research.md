<p class="lead">A single principle runs through our work: modular reasoning, the ability to understand and change one part of a system without grappling with the whole. We design the languages and interfaces that make modular reasoning possible, and we reframe software-analysis tasks so that this reasoning scales to millions of programs. Today the principle matters most for AI-enabled systems: we decompose learned models into modules and bring software-engineering rigor to the AI that society increasingly depends on, so it can be built, debugged, and trusted.</p>

<h2 class="research-header">Modularity and Modular Reasoning</h2>
<p>We design languages, interfaces, and contracts that let engineers reason about complex software one module at a time. This work introduced information-hiding and crosscutting interfaces for aspect-oriented design, Classpects unifying aspects and objects, Ptolemy's quantified typed events, capsule-oriented programming in Panini, and translucid contracts for modular verification.</p>

<h2 class="research-header">Software at Scale, with Boa</h2>
<p>The hard question behind Boa was not simply how to store hundreds of thousands of repositories, but how to express software-analysis tasks modularly so they can be decomposed and run across a massive collection of programs. Boa is the language and infrastructure that answers it: an analysis is written once, at a high level, and executed across the world's open-source code. The result is large-scale empirical software engineering that the whole community can use to study how software is built, modularized, and evolves.</p>

<h2 class="research-header">Modular and Dependable AI</h2>
<p>Modular reasoning extended to AI-enabled systems. We decompose learned models such as deep neural networks into reusable, replaceable modules, and we make AI software dependable through the first comprehensive study of deep-learning bugs, fault localization and repair for neural networks, and fairness across machine-learning pipelines.</p>
